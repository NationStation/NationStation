Vulnerability Criteria: How We Identify Those Most in Need

We employ a rigorous, data-driven assessment process to identify the most vulnerable families. Our vulnerability criteria are based on internationally recognized food security indicators and economic measures:

Economic Vulnerability

  • 73.3% of assessed households have monthly incomes below the extreme poverty line
  • 58.9% reported reducing meal sizes in the month prior to assessment due to lack of resources
  • 95.6% rely primarily on cash purchases for food, making them highly vulnerable to price inflation
  • 22% depend on assistance from organizations as their secondary food source

Food Security Indicators

  • Food Consumption Score (FCS): Measures dietary diversity and frequency of food consumption
  • Coping Strategy Index (CSI): Tracks negative behaviors families adopt to cope with food shortages
  • Household composition: Special consideration for households with infants, young children, pregnant/lactating women, and elderly members
  • Displacement status: Priority for internally displaced and war-affected families

Accountability & Protection Against Misuse

Nation Station implements comprehensive safeguards to ensure aid reaches intended beneficiaries and funds are used appropriately:

Digital Verification System

We use the Ona digital platform to conduct comprehensive household assessments, capturing:

  • Household composition and demographics
  • Income levels and sources
  • Current food sources and consumption patterns
  • Specific nutritional needs

All data is digitally recorded, time-stamped, and geo-tagged for verification.

Direct Distribution Model

Food assistance is delivered directly to families without intermediaries:

  • Identity verification at distribution points
  • Individual labeling of food boxes with recipient information
  • Beneficiary confirmation and signature collection
  • No cash transfers or vouchers that could be diverted

This eliminates opportunities for diversion to armed groups or other entities.

Monitoring & Documentation

Comprehensive tracking and accountability measures:

  • Photographic documentation of all preparation and distribution activities
  • Detailed inventory tracking of purchased food items
  • Distribution records with beneficiary confirmation
  • Regular team meetings to address operational challenges
  • Feedback collection from beneficiaries
Additional Safeguards

Our team operates from the Nation Station facility in Geitawi Area, Ashrafieh, Beirut, a secure and neutral location. We maintain strict political neutrality and do not engage with armed groups. All procurement is done through established commercial suppliers with proper documentation, and our staff members are community-based individuals with no affiliations to armed organizations.

Innovative Approach: Gender and Age-Sensitive Food Allocation

A key innovation of our 2025 project was the tailored food box design that considered the demographic composition of each household. This approach maximized nutritional impact and demonstrated our commitment to needs-based, dignified assistance.

Infants (0-3 years)

  • No high-salt items
  • No mortadella
  • Limited sugar
  • Infant formula included where needed
Children (3-9 years)
  • 60-80% of adult portions
  • Reduced spices
  • Increased nutrient density
  • Age-appropriate food items

  • Standard portions

  • Special items for women of reproductive age

2026 Program Plan: Expanded Food Security & Agricultural Initiatives

Building on our 2025 success, our 2026 program expands food assistance while integrating agricultural and nutrition education components. This comprehensive approach addresses both immediate food needs and long-term food security through sustainable practices.

Program Components

Objective: Provide sustained food assistance to 150 vulnerable families (approximately 675 individuals) through regular distribution of nutritionally balanced food boxes.

Key Features:
  • Increased reach: Expand from 90 to 150 families based on ongoing needs assessment
  • Extended duration: 12-month program (January - December 2026)
  • Distribution frequency: Bi-weekly distributions (24 rounds total)
  • Customized food boxes: Continue gender and age-sensitive allocation system
  • Fresh produce emphasis: Increase fresh vegetables, fruits, and dairy products to 40% of box contents
Food Box Contents:

Dry Goods:

  • Rice, pasta, and other grains
  • Legumes (lentils, beans, chickpeas)
  • Canned proteins (tuna, fava beans)
  • Oil, sugar, salt, spices
  • Fortified foods for children

Fresh Foods:

  • Seasonal vegetables (tomatoes, cucumbers, leafy greens)
  • Fresh fruits
  • Dairy products (yogurt, labneh, cheese)
  • Eggs
  • Fresh bread

Objective: Establish community gardens in beneficiary neighborhoods to supplement food boxes with fresh produce and build long-term food production capacity.

Activities:
  • Garden establishment: Set up 3-4 community garden sites in Burj, Geitawi, and Dahye areas
  • Crop selection: Focus on fast-growing, nutrient-dense vegetables (leafy greens, tomatoes, cucumbers, herbs)
  • Training program: Provide hands-on agricultural training to 50 beneficiary families
  • Tool and seed distribution: Supply basic gardening tools and quality seeds to participants
  • Technical support: Partner with agricultural experts for ongoing guidance
  • Harvest integration: Incorporate garden produce into food box distributions
Expected Outcomes:
  • Production of 500-800 kg of fresh vegetables over the growing season
  • 50 families gain practical agricultural skills
  • Reduced dependency on purchased fresh produce
  • Enhanced community cohesion through collaborative gardening
  • Foundation for income-generating activities through surplus sales

Objective: Empower beneficiaries with knowledge and skills to maximize nutritional value from available food resources.

Workshop Topics:
  • Balanced nutrition basics: Understanding food groups, dietary diversity, and nutritional needs for different age groups
  • Cooking demonstrations: Preparing nutritious meals from food box contents
  • Food preservation techniques: Drying, pickling, and storing fresh produce to extend shelf life
  • Budget-friendly cooking: Maximizing nutrition while minimizing costs
  • Infant and child nutrition: Special sessions for mothers on complementary feeding and child nutrition
  • Food safety and hygiene: Proper food handling and storage practices
Implementation:
  • Monthly workshops at Nation Station facility and community centers
  • Target: 100 participants (primarily women and mothers)
  • Hands-on, practical sessions with take-home materials
  • Partnership with nutritionists and culinary trainers

Objective: Support local agricultural economy while ensuring fresh, quality produce for beneficiaries.

Partnership Model:
  • Direct procurement: Purchase fresh produce directly from small-scale farmers in rural Lebanon
  • Fair pricing: Ensure farmers receive fair compensation for their products
  • Quality standards: Establish quality criteria for produce selection
  • Seasonal focus: Prioritize seasonal, locally-grown produce to reduce costs and environmental impact
  • Farmer network: Build relationships with 10-15 reliable farmer suppliers
Benefits:
  • Fresher, higher-quality produce for beneficiaries
  • Support for local agricultural livelihoods
  • Reduced transportation costs and environmental footprint
  • Strengthened rural-urban food supply chains

Objective: Ensure program effectiveness through systematic monitoring and data-driven adjustments.

Monitoring Activities:
  • Baseline and endline assessments: Measure Food Consumption Score and Coping Strategy Index at program start and end
  • Monthly distribution tracking: Record all food items distributed, beneficiary attendance, and feedback
  • Quarterly household surveys: Assess food security status, dietary diversity, and program satisfaction
  • Garden productivity monitoring: Track crop yields, participant engagement, and skill development
  • Workshop evaluation: Assess knowledge gain and behavior change from nutrition education
  • Financial tracking: Detailed budget monitoring and expense documentation
Reporting:
  • Monthly progress reports to donors
  • Quarterly impact assessments
  • Final comprehensive project report with lessons learned
  • Photo and video documentation throughout implementation
